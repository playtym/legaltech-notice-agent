What Is a Legal Notice?

A legal notice is a formal written communication sent by one party to another, informing them of a grievance or legal claim and demanding a specific remedy within a defined time period — failing which the sender will initiate legal proceedings.

In Indian law, a legal notice serves multiple purposes:

  • It creates an official, timestamped record of your complaint and the company's awareness of it
  • It gives the opposite party one last opportunity to resolve the dispute without court involvement
  • It is a legal prerequisite in certain cases (cheque bounce under Section 138 NI Act, some civil suits)
  • It becomes evidence if the matter escalates to court — courts look favourably on parties who made good-faith pre-litigation attempts
  • Companies take it seriously because a lawyer or Lawly-drafted notice signals you are ready to go to court

Who can send one? Any adult. You do not need to be a lawyer or hire one. The Consumer Protection Act 2019 explicitly allows self-representation, and a self-drafted or AI-drafted legal notice is fully admissible.

Standard Legal Notice Format — 9 Mandatory Elements

Every effective legal notice in India should contain the following in this order:

  1. Date and city of issue — top right or left, e.g., "New Delhi, 6 April 2026"
  2. Sender's name and full address (the person sending the notice)
  3. Recipient's full legal name and registered address — use the company's registered office address as per MCA21 (mca.gov.in)
  4. Subject line — brief, e.g., "Legal Notice for Refund of ₹8,500 — Order ID #ABC123"
  5. Facts of the dispute — what happened, chronologically, with dates and amounts
  6. Legal basis — the specific law or section being violated (e.g., "Section 2(11) of the Consumer Protection Act, 2019")
  7. Relief demanded — exact amounts and actions demanded from the recipient
  8. Deadline — "You are hereby called upon to comply within 15 days of receipt of this notice"
  9. Consequences — "Failure to comply will compel me to initiate proceedings before the appropriate Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission / High Court, with claims for additional compensation and litigation costs at your risk."

Followed by: Yours faithfully, [Name, Signature, Date]

FACTS: On [Date of Purchase], I purchased [product/service name] from your platform / store for a consideration of ₹[Amount] vide Order ID / Invoice No. [X]. 2. DEFICIENCY: [Describe what went wrong — e.g., the product was defective / was not delivered / did not match description / service was not rendered as promised]. This constitutes a defect in goods under Section 2(10) / deficiency in service under Section 2(11) of the Consumer Protection Act, 2019. 3. ATTEMPTS TO RESOLVE: I reported this issue to your customer care on [date] (Ticket/Reference No.: [X]) and subsequently to your Grievance Officer on [date]. To date, no satisfactory resolution has been provided, which further constitutes a continuing deficiency under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019. 4. DEMAND: I hereby call upon you to, within 15 (fifteen) days from the receipt of this notice: (a) Refund the full amount of ₹[Amount] to my original payment instrument; (b) Pay compensation of ₹[Amount] for mental agony, inconvenience, and loss caused; and (c) Bear the cost of this legal notice ₹[Amount]. 5. CONSEQUENCES: Failure to comply within the stipulated period will leave me with no alternative but to initiate proceedings before the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, [Your City], under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, and to claim the above amounts along with additional relief including punitive damages — entirely at your risk as to cost. Yours faithfully, [Your Name] [Signature] Date: 6 April 2026

Free Template [City], [Date] From: [Payee's Full Name] [Address] To: [Drawer's Full Name] [Address] Subject: LEGAL NOTICE UNDER SECTION 138 OF THE NEGOTIABLE INSTRUMENTS ACT, 1881 Sir / Madam, 1. You issued cheque no. [Cheque No.] dated [Date] drawn on [Bank Name, Branch] for ₹[Amount] in favour of the undersigned towards [reason — e.g., repayment of loan / payment for services]. 2. On presentation, the said cheque was returned unpaid by your bank on [Date] with the memo "[Reason — e.g., Insufficient Funds / Account Closed]". The dishonour slip is enclosed. 3. This notice is issued within 30 days of receipt of the bank memo, as required under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881. 4. You are hereby called upon to pay the cheque amount of ₹[Amount] within 15 days of receipt of this notice. 5. Failure to make payment within the stipulated 15 days will compel me to file a criminal complaint against you under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, which is punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years or a fine up to twice the cheque amount or both — without further notice. Yours faithfully, [Your Name, Signature, Date] Note: Retain the original cheque, bank return memo, and the postal receipt for this notice — all are essential evidence in a Section 138 case.

⚠️ Section 138 timeline is strict. This notice must be sent within 30 days of receiving the bank's cheque return memo. If you miss this window, you lose the right to file a criminal complaint. Contact Lawly immediately if your cheque was recently returned.

How to Send a Legal Notice in India — Step by Step

  1. Draft the notice — using Lawly's AI tool, a lawyer, or using the templates above.
  2. Print on plain paper — A4, no special stamp paper needed for most notices.
  3. Sign and date it.
  4. Send by Registered Post with Acknowledge Due (RPAD) at any India Post office. Cost: ₹20–₹50. Ask for the "Speed Post Registered" or "Regd. A/D" option. Keep the postal receipt and the green acknowledgement card when it returns.
  5. Also send by email to the Grievance Officer's official email address. Keep the outbox delivery record and request a read receipt.
  6. Keep copies of everything — the notice, postal receipt, A/D card, email delivery confirmation.

Send to: the company's registered office address (find it on MCA21 master data), not a warehouse or call centre address. For individuals, send to their home address or last known address.

Is a Legal Notice Sent by Email Valid in India?

Yes — with caveats. Indian courts including the Supreme Court and various High Courts have ruled that emails and WhatsApp messages can serve as valid legal communications, provided the sender can prove:

  • The email was delivered — use a read receipt, or a follow-up email confirmed by the recipient
  • It was sent to the correct, official email address of the company/person
  • The content of the email notice was complete and complied with the formal requirements

However, if the matter escalates to court, a registered post receipt is much harder to dispute — the company cannot claim they never received it. Best practice: send by both email AND RPAD simultaneously.

Frequently Asked Questions — Legal Notice Format India

What is the format of a legal notice in India?▼
A standard legal notice contains: (1) Date and city, (2) Sender's name and address, (3) Recipient's registered name and address, (4) Subject line, (5) Facts of dispute with dates and amounts, (6) Legal basis — the specific law/section, (7) Relief demanded with amounts, (8) Deadline (15–30 days), (9) Consequences of non-compliance, (10) Sender's signature. No special paper or court stamp needed for most notices.
Can a legal notice be sent by email in India?▼
Yes — courts accept email as valid legal communication if you can prove delivery (read receipt or confirmation). However, registered post (RPAD) is more reliable in court because it's harder for the recipient to deny. Best practice: send by both email AND RPAD simultaneously.
Is it mandatory to send a legal notice before filing a consumer complaint?▼
Not mandatory for EDAAKHIL or NCH filings. However, it is compulsory for cheque bounce cases (Section 138 NI Act — 15-day notice is legally required). For consumer cases, it's strongly advisable: ~60% of disputes settle after a legal notice; it also creates evidence that you gave the company a chance to resolve before court.
What is the response time for a legal notice in India?▼
You set the deadline yourself in the notice — typically 15 days for e-commerce refunds and 30 days for service providers. The 15-day period for cheque bounce is mandated by law. There is no law that forces a company to respond within any particular time to a consumer legal notice — but non-response is admissible evidence of bad faith in court.
What happens if the company doesn't reply to a legal notice?▼
Non-response is powerful evidence for you. File immediately on EDAAKHIL (consumer court) or the appropriate court — don't send a second notice, that signals weakness. For cheque bounce, non-payment after the 15-day period allows you to file a Section 138 criminal complaint. Consumer courts regularly grant ex-parte orders in favour of complainants when companies don't respond.
Can I draft my own legal notice without a lawyer?▼
Yes. No law requires a legal notice to be drafted by an advocate. A self-drafted or AI-drafted notice is fully valid. Using Lawly, you can generate a complete, citation-accurate legal notice in under 2 minutes by answering a few questions. The notices cite exact sections of Indian law applicable to your dispute.
How much does a legal notice cost in India?▼
Via Lawly: ₹199 to draft + ₹20–50 registered post = ~₹250 total. Via a local lawyer: ₹500–₹5,000. Via a senior lawyer for complex matters: ₹5,000–₹20,000+. The drafting cost is claimable — include it as part of your "cost of litigation" demand in the notice itself.
What is the legal notice format for a consumer complaint specifically?▼
For a consumer complaint notice: cite Section 2(10) (defect in goods) or 2(11) (deficiency of service) of the Consumer Protection Act 2019, and if applicable the Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules 2020. State the purchase details, what went wrong, prior attempts to resolve, and demand: (a) refund, (b) compensation for mental agony, (c) litigation costs. Set a 15-day deadline.
