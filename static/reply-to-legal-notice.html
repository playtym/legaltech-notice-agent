How to Reply to a Legal Notice in India (2026)

Receiving a legal notice can feel alarming — but it is not a court summons. No one is being arrested. No court case has started yet. It is a formal demand from another party, and you have time to respond thoughtfully. Here is exactly what to do.

First thing to know: A legal notice is sent before any lawsuit. Responding to it well — even briefly — is always better than ignoring it. Most disputes that start with a legal notice are resolved without ever reaching court.

Step 1: Read It Calmly and Systematically

When you receive a legal notice by Registered Post, courier, or email, do this before anything else:

  • Note the date of the notice and the date you received it
  • Identify: who sent it (sender + their lawyer, if through a lawyer), the delivery address, and whether it is addressed to you personally or your company
  • Identify the core claim — what are they saying you did or failed to do?
  • Identify the demand — what amount or action do they want?
  • Identify the law sections cited
  • Note the deadline they have given you to respond

If the notice mentions Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act (cheque bounce) — this is time-critical. They have a 30-day window to send this notice, and you have 15 days from receipt to pay or the criminal offence is deemed complete. If the cheque was genuinely dishonoured on your account, consult a lawyer immediately.

If the notice mentions Section 80 CPC (government/public body as sender) — this has specific procedural requirements. Consult a lawyer before replying.

Step 2: Verify the Notice is Legitimate

Before responding, verify:

  • Is the sender's name and address real? Look up the law firm if mentioned.
  • Did it arrive by Registered Post AD or a known courier? Photocopied notices with no postal proof are sometimes used as bluffs.
  • Does it have a specific, factual claim — or is it vague and threatening? Legitimate legal notices are specific about amounts, dates, and law sections.

Step 3: Determine Your Position

You have three options:

  • Option A — The claim is valid and you intend to settle: Contact the sender or their lawyer and negotiate settlement before the deadline. Get the settlement in writing. This is the fastest and cheapest outcome.
  • Option B — The claim is partly valid: Acknowledge what is correct, dispute what is incorrect with your evidence. A partial settlement may be appropriate.
  • Option C — The claim is factually wrong: Send a clear written reply disputing the facts with your evidence (receipts, screenshots, emails, bank statements).

Step 4: Draft Your Reply — Dos and Don'ts

✓ Do:

  • Respond in writing, formally
  • Acknowledge receipt of the notice
  • State your position factually and clearly
  • Cite specific evidence (dates, amounts, reference numbers)
  • Use a professional, neutral tone
  • Send by Registered Post AD
  • Keep a copy of everything

✗ Don't:

  • Ignore the notice entirely
  • Write emotionally or threateningly
  • Make admissions you don't intend (e.g., "I'm sorry for the delay")
  • Use WhatsApp or informal channels as your only response
  • Reveal privileged information
  • Make partial payments without documenting them as "without prejudice"
  • Promise to pay by a date you can't meet

Step 5: The Reply Format

A proper reply to a legal notice follows this structure:

Date: [Date of your reply] To, [Sender's Name] [Sender's Address — exactly as it appears on the notice] [Through their advocate, if applicable] Subject: Reply to Legal Notice dated [Date of the notice] — [Brief Subject] Sir/Madam, I have received your legal notice dated [Date] sent by [Registered Post / Email / Courier]. I hereby provide my reply to the claims made therein. [Paragraph 1: Brief acknowledgement of the notice and your identity] I am [Your Name], [your address]. I am in receipt of the above-mentioned notice. [Paragraph 2: Your factual position — admit, dispute, or partially admit] With regard to the claims made in the notice: [If disputing:] The claim that [state their claim] is factually incorrect. On [date], I [state what actually happened, with evidence reference — e.g., "I made the payment of ₹X via NEFT on [date], UTR No. XXXXXXXXX, attached as Exhibit A"]. The demand for ₹[amount] is therefore unsustainable and baseless. [If admitting:] I acknowledge that an amount of ₹[X] is outstanding. However, [state mitigating circumstances if any]. I am willing to settle this matter by [proposed resolution]. [Paragraph 3: State your position on the demand] In view of the above, [the demand made in your notice is disputed / I am agreeable to settling the undisputed amount of ₹X / I will be taking appropriate legal advice and shall communicate my position within [X] days]. [Paragraph 4: Without-prejudice caveat if making a partial offer] Any payment made hereunder is strictly without prejudice to my rights and does not constitute an admission of the full claim. Yours faithfully, [Your Full Name] [Your Address] [Your Contact Number / Email] Enclosures: 1. [List of documents attached, e.g., NEFT receipt, email screenshots]

Step 6: Send — And Keep Proof

  1. Print and sign the reply

    Print on plain paper (letterhead if you have one). Sign it.

  2. Attach all evidence as Annexures

    Label clearly: Annexure A, Annexure B, etc. Make 3 copies: one to send, one for your records, one if the sender has a lawyer (send to lawyer's address too if stated on the notice).

  3. Send by Registered Post AD

    Send to the sender's address (and their lawyer's address if notice was sent through a lawyer). Get the receipt stamped. Keep the receipt. When the ADC card returns, file it with your reply copy.

  4. Send a copy by email too

    If the notice provided an email address (sender or their lawyer), email a scan of the signed reply with all annexures. This creates an additional timestamp.

  5. File everything safely

    Keep: original notice envelope, the notice itself, your reply, your postal receipt, the returned ADC card, all annexures. This is your evidence file if the matter goes to court.

What Happens After You Reply?

  • If both parties agree on terms, settle with a written settlement agreement
  • If the sender disagrees with your reply, they may send a counter-notice or proceed to court
  • Either way, you have protected yourself on record — your reply shows you engaged in good faith
  • If a case is filed against you subsequently, your reply and the sender's notice become part of the case record

Special Situations

If you received a cheque bounce legal notice (Section 138):

This is the most time-critical situation. You have 15 days from receipt of the notice to pay the full cheque amount plus costs. If you pay within 15 days, the criminal offence does not materialise. If you do not pay, the sender can file a criminal complaint within 30 days of the expiry of the 15-day period. Consult a lawyer immediately — the criminal consequences (up to 2 years imprisonment) are serious.

If you received a legal notice from a company you dealt with as a consumer:

Companies sometimes send legal notices to consumers to intimidate them from pursuing legitimate complaints. If you are confident in your consumer rights claim, you can both: (a) reply to their notice disputing their claims, AND (b) simultaneously file your own consumer complaint on EDAAKHIL. The Consumer Forum will not be swayed by the company's notice.

If you received a legal notice demanding more than you owe:

Clearly dispute the inflated figure in your reply with your calculation and evidence. Make any undisputed payment "without prejudice" (use that exact phrase in your payment transfer description and reply). This prevents the undisputed portion from causing further delay while protecting you on the disputed amount.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it mandatory to reply to a legal notice in India?
No — there is no law that requires you to reply to a private legal notice. However, ignoring it is strategically unwise. If a court case follows, your silence will be presented as evidence of bad faith or admission of the claim. Replying — even to dispute — protects your position on record.

How many days do I have to reply to a legal notice?
The deadline is stated within the notice itself, typically 15–30 days. There is no fixed statutory deadline for replying to a private legal notice (unlike Section 138 NI Act, where the 15-day payment window is statutory). However, you should reply before the sender's stated deadline. If you need more time to gather facts, send a brief acknowledgement letter within the deadline period.

What happens if I ignore a legal notice?
Ignoring a legal notice does not trigger any automatic legal consequence on its own. However, the sender will almost certainly file the threatened case — and your silence will be used against you. Courts treat deliberate non-response as an aggravating factor when awarding compensation or costs.

Can I admit to part of the claim and dispute the rest?
Yes — and this is often the wisest approach. Acknowledge the undisputed portion and clearly dispute the rest with your evidence. Use the phrase "without prejudice" if you make any payment to prevent it being construed as a full admission.

Should I hire a lawyer to reply to a legal notice?
Not necessarily for routine consumer disputes or simple money recovery matters. However, for Section 138 cheque bounce notices, property disputes, defamation claims, employment termination notices, or any matter involving potential criminal proceedings — consult a lawyer. The cost of a wrong response can be significantly higher than the lawyer's fee.
